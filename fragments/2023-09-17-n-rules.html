n Rules for Life

2025-07-19

  1. The mind, like nature, abhors a vacuum. Deprived of real suffering, it fabricates troubles of its own, magnifying the trivial until it becomes unbearable. [Unknown]
  2. The absence of hardship does not bring peace; it breeds restlessness. [Unknown]
  3. A single lie lingers, no matter how many truths try to wash it away. [Unknown]
  4. When nothing is done, everything is done (Tzu & Needleman, 1989), thus do absolutely nothing unless it must be done absolutely.
  5. The most essential ingredient in any recipe is the appetite of those to whom the meal is served. [Unknown]
  6. Always be truthful in your actions, even when the truth is inconvenient. However, never tell the truth when dealing with a fool, for only a fool is capable of distorting and destroying the truth. [Unknown]
  7. The universe operates on principles that prevent total destruction, but these same principles also limit easy solutions to life’s problems. [Unknown]
  8. If we read too quickly or too slowly, we understand nothing. The same applies to wine—too much or too little. Deprive him entirely, and he cannot grasp the truth; give him too much, and it slips away just the same. (Pascal & Eliot, 2011)
  9. We don’t depend only on our own strength; we balance two opposing flaws, like maintaining balance with conflicting forces. If we remove one, we fall into the other. (Pascal & Eliot, 2011)
  10. Aristoteles’s maxim: We should not expect precision from a theory beyond what its subject matter will bear. If poetry is not math, neither is virtue and vice. (James, 2014)
  11. Analogy is not equivalency. The former provides insight, the latter truth. [Unknown]
  12. Learn to stop at 80% limit, while eating, talking and working. [Unknown]
  13. If you cannot solve the proposed problem, try to solve first some related problem. Polya & Conway (2014)
  14. Depth over breadth. Refine fundamentals and grow incrementally. (Waitzkin, 2007)
  15. Work but do not take credit, create but do not claim, lead but do not interfere. (Tzu & Needleman, 1989)
  16. Concision is to mind what cleanliness is to the body. Dalrymple (2021)
  17. Life isn’t a problem to be solved, find the flow state in everything you do, and you will live a good life. (García et al., 2017).
  18. Organizations default to bureaucracy. People default to distraction.
  19. Roughly 80% of outcomes come from 20% of causes (see Pareto principle).
  20. The square root of the number of people in a productive domain produce half of its total output. (Price’s Law)
  21. When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure (see Goodhart’s Law)
  22. Work expands to fill the time available for its completion (see Parkinson’s law).
  23. Don’t be a perfectionist. The desire for perfection could be a deadly weakness by György Sebők in Denk (2022).
  24. Acute differences positively manifest unity and that you cannot conceive the one without the other. Watts (2007, p. 104) (see Ji-ji-mu-ge principle: all things and events are mutually interpenetrating and interdependent. Watts (2007, p. 120)).
  25. You are what you think: Keep an untroubled spirit; for all things must bow to Nature’s Law. Aurelius (2003).
  26. Accept things as they are whether agreeable or disagreeable. Suzuki (2020).
  27. There’s an extremely limited number of ways of being right and an almost infinite number of ways to be wrong. Phillips (2020).
  28. Focus not so much on words but on actual behavior. (Don’t watch his mouth, watch his hands. – Adler’s dictum).
  29. Make everything as simple as possible, but not simpler. (Albert Einstein).
  30. Perceive those things which cannot be seen (e.g. pay attention even to trifle). And do nothing which is of no use. (Miyamoto Musashi).
  31. Life is often capricious, unfair and sometimes cruel. Murakami (2022, p. 126).
  32. Non-stupid people always underestimate the damaging power of stupid individuals. In particular, non-stupid people constantly forget that at all times and places and under any circumstances to deal and/or associate with stupid people infallibly turns out to be a costly mistake. Cipolla (2019).
  33. Never lose sight of ultimate purpose, your physical and mental health: You’re sane and breathing this minute and may be gone forever the next minute. (Unknown, probably Aurelius) Maintain your physical fitness and sanity.
  34. Putting things off is the biggest waste of life: it snatches away each day as it comes, and denies us the present by promising future. The whole future lies in uncertainty. Live immediately. Seneca (1997, p. 13).
  35. Practice any art, music, singing, dancing, acting, drawing, painting, sculpting, poetry, fiction, essays, reportage, no matter how well or badly, not to get money and fame, but to experience becoming, to find out what’s inside you, to make your soul grow. – Kurt Vonnegut.
  36. No hurry, no pause: When you rush, time shrinks. When you’re relaxed, time expands. This is true even though it’s very hard for the mind to get. When you’re relaxed and doing something willingly, you’re participating in life. (Jon Schreiber, Breema Center)
  37. Reflect on what you’ve read and written: Information becomes knowledge by reflection. Reflection cultivates intuition. Abundant intuition makes up for lack of information thereby enabling you operate where your knowledge is limited. (Unknown).
  38. Give your attention proportioned to the merits of the case: Sometimes full attention, sometimes none. (Unknown).
  39. Do not try to stay on top of things (you cannot). Instead, try to get to the bottom of things. (This kind of success, while not guaranteed, will be more fulfilling than the former).
  40. It is more important to invest in good people than in good ideas. (bet on the jockey not the horse).
  41. One who makes a show of valor is hated by others, even he achieves something, it is not praised but denied. (Kumazawa Banzan).
  42. In the short term, you are as good as your intensity. In the long term, you are only as good as your consistency.
  43. We read the same facts differently. For an optimist philosopher the universe spells victory, for a pessimist, defeat. (William James, from Pragmatism and Other Writings).
